## What is cointegration?

Cointegration is a way to describe two or more price series that can each wander over time, but still maintain a stable long-run relationship. If `x_t` and `y_t` are both non-stationary, they may still be cointegrated if a linear combination like `x_t - beta y_t` is stationary. In trading terms, that means the absolute prices do not need to revert, but the properly hedged spread between them does tend to move back toward equilibrium.

That matters because most equity pairs or stock-factor relationships share common drivers. A stock may move with the market, with its sector, and with its own idiosyncratic noise. A cointegration-style strategy tries to strip out the common market and sector components first, then trade the residual deviation. The core assumption is that this residual dislocation is temporary and mean-reverting, even if the raw stock price itself is not.

## Application

`v28` does not trade a raw stock-vs-sector price spread. Instead, it first removes market and sector structure from each stock's daily returns, then mean-reverts the residual. In [v28_notebook.py](/Users/jplatta/repos/quant_studies/experiments/xsect-mean-rev-cointegration/v28_notebook.py#L60), the key calculation is a rolling two-factor residualization over `126` days. For each stock return series `r_i`, the code estimates market beta as `beta_m = Cov(r_i, r_m) / Var(r_m)` and sector beta on market-stripped returns as `beta_s = Cov(r_i - beta_m r_m, r_s - beta_sm r_m) / Var(r_s - beta_sm r_m)`, where `r_m` is `SPY` and `r_s` is the stock's mapped sector ETF. The residual return is then `eps_i = r_i - beta_m r_m - beta_s (r_s - beta_sm r_m)`; in code this is `stock_ex_market - sector_beta * sector_ex_market` at lines `80`-`85`.

The actual mean-reversion signal is built from the cumulative short-horizon residual move, not from raw returns. At lines `86`-`90`, the script sums residual returns over an `8`-day window, `residual_signal = sum_{t-7}^t eps_i`, then standardizes that with a rolling z-score, `z_i = (residual_signal - rolling_mean) / rolling_std`, clipped to `[-3, 3]`. The trade signal is `-z_i` at lines `93`-`103`, so names that have outperformed on a residual basis become shorts and underperformed names become longs. This is the practical “cointegration” step in `v28`: the strategy assumes the stock's properly hedged relative path should revert after a temporary dislocation.

The rest of the pipeline is there to keep the signal cross-sectional and benchmark-light. The script de-means the signal globally and again within sector at lines `106`-`122`, then filters to liquid, tradeable names and removes weak-volume names with `.add_volume_zscore_filter(window=30, min_zscore_quantile=0.8)` at line `184`. After building a `10` by `10` long/short book, it applies an explicit rolling beta neutralizer at lines `125`-`166`, which projects the active weights off the estimated `SPY` beta vector using `w' = w - ((w · beta) / (beta · beta)) beta`, then renormalizes gross exposure. In [build_v28_study()](/Users/jplatta/repos/quant_studies/experiments/xsect-mean-rev-cointegration/v28_notebook.py#L169), that full sequence is what lets `v28` keep Sharpe high while bringing benchmark correlation down into the target range.
