# Implementation Notes

The final portfolio was constructed using liquid large-cap U.S. equities constrained to the top-300 names by liquidity. This substantially improves implementation feasibility relative to small-cap or microstructure-sensitive statistical arbitrage strategies. The resulting portfolio exhibited moderate aggregate turnover despite containing several shorter-horizon reversal sleeves, primarily because turnover was diversified across heterogeneous signal families and conditioning regimes.

Transaction cost analysis showed that the portfolio remained profitable across a wide range of assumed trading costs. While several individual sleeves were highly cost-sensitive, diversification materially stabilized implementation performance at the portfolio level. In particular, the distance-pairs and event-driven reversal sleeves generated the largest turnover burden and therefore contributed most heavily to implementation drag. The strongest portfolio configurations nevertheless retained Sharpe ratios above 1.5 even under materially higher transaction cost assumptions, suggesting the portfolio was not solely dependent on unrealistically low execution frictions.

Turnover attribution analysis revealed meaningful differences between sleeve types. Residualized mean-reversion sleeves and slower-horizon reversal sleeves exhibited relatively low turnover and appeared operationally scalable. In contrast, faster distance-pairs sleeves and event-driven gap-reversion sleeves generated substantially higher trading activity and would likely represent the primary execution bottlenecks in live deployment. The highest-turnover distance-pairs sleeve in particular appeared to drive a disproportionate share of overall implementation complexity.

The conditioning framework also materially improved implementation quality. Conditioning filters reduced unnecessary trading during unfavorable market regimes and frequently lowered turnover while improving post-cost Sharpe ratios. Volatility contraction filters, breadth filters, and trend-aware scaling were especially effective at stabilizing several mean-reversion sleeves. This suggests that conditioning served not only as a return enhancement mechanism, but also as an implementation control mechanism.

Capacity considerations remain important despite the liquid universe selection. Short-horizon cross-sectional reversal strategies are vulnerable to crowding, slippage, and execution degradation as capital scales. Distance-pairs trading in particular may become increasingly difficult to scale due to correlated positioning and spread convergence effects during stressed periods. While the portfolio appears feasible at moderate institutional scale, live implementation would likely require careful execution scheduling, trade crossing, borrow management, and dynamic participation controls.

Leverage analysis demonstrated that the portfolio’s relatively modest unlevered return profile could be transformed into a more economically meaningful return stream through volatility targeting. Scaling the portfolio to a 10% target volatility substantially increased cumulative returns while maintaining moderate drawdowns and stable diversification characteristics. Importantly, leverage did not appear to destabilize the portfolio structure, suggesting that diversification between sleeves remained effective after scaling. This behavior is consistent with many market-neutral and statistical arbitrage strategies, where high Sharpe ratios but modest standalone returns are typically enhanced through controlled leverage and volatility targeting.
